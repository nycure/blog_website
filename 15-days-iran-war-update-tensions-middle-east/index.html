It seeks to consolidate its regional influence and push back against perceived foreign interference. Its leadership emphasizes resilience against sanctions and military threats.

  • United States: Seeks to contain Iran's regional influence, prevent nuclear proliferation, and ensure the security of its allies. It often employs a combination of sanctions, military deterrence, and diplomatic pressure to achieve its objectives. The US stance is largely shaped by concerns over maritime security and the protection of global trade routes.

  • Regional Allies (e.g., Saudi Arabia, UAE, Israel): Share concerns about Iran's ballistic missile program, support for proxy groups, and regional ambitions. These nations often advocate for stronger international action against Iran and maintain their own robust defense capabilities. Their security doctrines are heavily influenced by the perceived threat from Iran.

  • European Union: Emphasizes diplomatic solutions, adherence to international agreements, and de-escalation. The EU often seeks to act as a mediator, advocating for a return to the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) and emphasizing multilateral engagement. Their focus is on preserving regional stability and preventing wider conflict.

  • Russia and China: Often critical of Western unilateralism, they maintain economic and strategic ties with Iran. They tend to advocate for dialogue and oppose what they view as excessive external interference in the region. Their influence often serves as a counterbalance to Western pressures.

The divergent interests and entrenched positions of these key players underscore the difficulty in forging a unified path forward. Each actor operates within a complex web of historical context, current geopolitical realities, and future aspirations, making negotiations a delicate balancing act.
