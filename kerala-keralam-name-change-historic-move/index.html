Kerala to Keralam: Unpacking the Historic Name Change Bid

A New Identity: The Proposed Name Change of Kerala to Keralam

In a truly historic move, the Union Cabinet recently approved a significant bid to formally change the name of the southern Indian state of Kerala to 'Keralam', a decision that resonates deeply with linguistic identity and historical pride. This eagerly anticipated decision, cleared on February 24, 2026, marks a pivotal moment in the state's ongoing quest to align its constitutional name with its deeply ingrained Malayalam linguistic heritage. The journey to effectuate this name change of Kerala to Keralam has involved unanimous resolutions from the state's legislative assembly and careful navigation of India's constitutional framework. For many, this is more than just an alteration of letters; it represents a reaffirmation of the state's authentic cultural and historical self. This initiative underscores a broader trend in India of states reclaiming names that better represent their indigenous heritage and linguistic distinctiveness. The proposed transition from 'Kerala' to 'Keralam' reflects a long-standing aspiration to correct what is widely considered an anglicized version of the state's name, a legacy from the colonial era. While 'Kerala' has been the official nomenclature for decades, the term 'Keralam' is the native and historically resonant name used in the Malayalam language, spoken by the vast majority of the state's population. This initiative underscores a broader trend in India of states reclaiming names that better represent their indigenous heritage and linguistic distinctiveness. The process now moves into the crucial parliamentary phase, requiring legislative approval to finalize this historic transformation.

Tracing the Roots: The Historical and Linguistic Significance of Keralam

The name 'Keralam' holds profound historical and linguistic significance for the people of the state. Etymologically, scholars often link 'Keralam' to ancient roots, with some tracing it to 'Cheram,' a reference to the revered Chera dynasty that historically ruled the region. The earliest epigraphic mention, found in Rock Edict II of Emperor Ashoka around 257 BCE, refers to "Keralaputra," often interpreted as "son of Kerala" or a reference to the Chera rulers. German scholar Herman Gundert, who compiled the first Malayalam-English dictionary, noted 'keram' as the Kannada form of 'cheram' and described 'Keralam' as the region between Gokarnam and Kanyakumari. The root 'cher' is understood to mean "to join," forming "Cheralam," with "alam" denoting land or region, signifying a united land.

The concept of 'Keralam' is intrinsically tied to the Malayalam language itself. The state was formed on linguistic lines on November 1, 1956, a date celebrated annually as 'Kerala Piravi' (Kerala Formation Day). The demand for a united 'Keralam' for Malayalam-speaking people has been a strong sentiment since the days of India's freedom struggle, highlighting the deep desire for linguistic and cultural unification. Therefore, for many, 'Keralam' is not merely an alternative spelling but the authentic, traditional, and culturally accurate designation for their homeland, embodying centuries of history and identity.

The Legislative Journey: From State Resolution to Union Cabinet Approval

The initiative for the name change of Kerala to Keralam has been a persistent one, championed by the state government. The process gained significant momentum with two unanimous resolutions passed by the Kerala Legislative Assembly. The first resolution was adopted in August 2023, moved by Chief Minister Pinarayi Vijayan. This initial proposal, however, was returned by the Union Home Ministry due to technical discrepancies, as it had sought changes not only in the First Schedule but also across all languages listed in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution.

Addressing these procedural issues, the Kerala Assembly passed a revised, unanimous resolution on June 24, 2024. This subsequent resolution specifically requested the Union government to take urgent steps under Article 3 of the Constitution to amend only the First Schedule, thereby replacing 'Kerala' with 'Keralam' and aligning the constitutional name with its Malayalam equivalent. Following the state government's formal request and careful examination by the Ministry of Home Affairs, the proposal received the crucial endorsement of the Union Cabinet on February 24, 2026. Union Minister Ashwini Vaishnaw confirmed the Cabinet's approval, signaling a major step forward in this significant renaming process.

Constitutional Process: How States Alter Their Names in India

The renaming of a state in India is a multi-step constitutional process governed primarily by Article 3 of the Constitution. This article grants Parliament the power to form new states, alter areas, boundaries, and change the name of any existing state. The procedure typically begins with a resolution passed by the State Legislative Assembly, recommending the proposed name change. While not constitutionally mandatory, such a resolution carries significant political and cultural weight, demonstrating the popular will of the state.

Following the state's resolution, the Union government examines the request, with the Ministry of Home Affairs playing a key role in scrutinizing its constitutional and administrative implications. The draft Cabinet note is circulated to relevant departments like Legal Affairs and Legislative Department under the Ministry of Law and Justice for their concurrence and legal vetting. After the Union Cabinet's approval, as seen in Kerala's case, the President of India recommends the introduction of a Bill (e.g., the Kerala (Alteration of Name) Bill, 2026) in Parliament. Crucially, under the proviso to Article 3, if the Bill affects the name, area, or boundaries of a state, it must first be referred by the President to the concerned State Legislature to express its views within a specified period. It is important to note that Parliament is not bound by the state's opinion, but the referral is a mandatory procedural step to ensure federal consultation.

Once the state's views are received (or the stipulated period expires), the Bill is introduced in either the Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha. It must be passed by a simple majority of members present and voting in both houses. Renaming a state does not require a special majority under Article 368; it is treated as an ordinary legislative process. After being passed by both Houses, the Bill is sent to the President for final assent. Once signed, it becomes law, the name change is notified in the Official Gazette, and the First Schedule of the Constitution is amended to reflect the new name. Article 4 permits these supplemental and consequential changes through laws made under Article 3, ensuring all necessary legal adjustments are seamlessly incorporated.

Precedents: Other States That Changed Their Names

India has a rich history of states and cities undergoing name changes since Independence, often to shed colonial legacies or to better reflect linguistic and cultural identities. These renamings are not merely cosmetic; they represent the sentiments and historical pride of the people in those regions, contributing to the vibrant tapestry that makes India a symphony of wonders. For instance, Madras State was renamed Tamil Nadu in 1969 to honor Tamil culture and language, following a significant linguistic movement. Similarly, Mysore became Karnataka in 1973, representing its Kannada-speaking regions and consolidating a distinct identity.

Other notable examples include Uttaranchal becoming Uttarakhand in 2007, often referred to as 'Devbhoomi' or "Land of Gods," a change that affirmed its spiritual and geographical distinctiveness. Orissa was renamed Odisha in 2011 through a constitutional amendment, also changing the name of its language from Oriya to Odia, aligning with the prevalent Odia language pronunciation and identity. Pondicherry was renamed Puducherry in 2006. Even before these, Travancore-Cochin was merged and renamed Kerala in 1956, a fact that makes the current proposed change to 'Keralam' a continuation of the state's linguistic and cultural evolution. These precedents demonstrate a consistent pattern of respecting regional aspirations and historical identity through strategic and culturally significant name changes across the Indian subcontinent.

Public and Political Reactions to the Name Change of Kerala to Keralam

The proposal for the name change of Kerala to Keralam has garnered broad support within the state. Chief Minister Pinarayi Vijayan has consistently emphasized that 'Keralam' is the historical Malayalam name, while 'Kerala' emerged during British rule for administrative convenience. The unanimous passage of resolutions in the Kerala Assembly underscores the widespread political consensus on this matter within the state, reflecting a unified cultural sentiment. Notably, the state unit of the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), through its president Rajeev Chandrasekhar, also extended support to the move, citing civilizational and linguistic heritage. This rare political convergence highlights the deep cultural resonance of the name 'Keralam' across party lines, transcending typical ideological divides.

However, the path to name changes is not always swift or uniform across all states. For instance, West Bengal has repeatedly sought to change its name to 'Bangla' or 'Paschim Banga' through multiple assembly resolutions since 2016, but its proposals remain pending at the Centre. West Bengal Chief Minister Mamata Banerjee, reacting to the approval of Kerala's renaming, alleged political considerations behind the swift nod for Kerala, suggesting an "understanding between the BJP and the CPM." This comparison highlights the discretionary nature of central government approvals and the political dynamics that can influence such processes, even when states pass unanimous resolutions. Despite such political observations, the overwhelming sentiment in Kerala is one of affirmation and pride in reclaiming a name deeply rooted in its heritage.

The Broader Impact of the Renaming

The official adoption of 'Keralam' is expected to have a largely symbolic but deeply significant impact, resonating far beyond a mere cosmetic alteration. Primarily, it will serve to profoundly reinforce the state's linguistic and cultural identity, aligning its official name with the authentic term used by its people in their native Malayalam tongue. This change is widely viewed as a profound restoration of heritage, meticulously correcting an anglicized nomenclature inherited from colonial rule and celebrating Malayalam as the foundational identity of the state. It is a powerful affirmation of self-determination, strengthening regional pride and distinguishing Kerala's unique place within the diverse mosaic of Indian states.

From an administrative perspective, the logistical implications, while substantial, are manageable. The immediate impact on day-to-day governance structures, state boundaries, or the validity of existing official documents like passports, Aadhaar cards, and educational certificates is expected to be minimal, as provisions will be made for continuity. However, the change will necessitate a comprehensive, phased update of all official records, departmental stationery, public signage, digital government portals, legal frameworks, and educational curricula over time. This extensive administrative process will require careful coordination across various government departments and potentially incur significant costs for rebranding and reprinting materials. Internationally, the state's name will need to be updated in diplomatic records and geographical references, though the colloquial 'Kerala' may persist in informal contexts for a period.

Economically, beyond potential rebranding efforts and a momentary spotlight on the state, significant direct impacts are not anticipated. The state's vibrant tourism industry, often promoted with the tagline 'God's Own Country', is unlikely to see a fundamental shift, though marketing materials will eventually adopt the new name. The essence of this change lies overwhelmingly in its cultural, historical, and linguistic significance, rather than its administrative burden or direct economic ramifications. It marks a powerful statement about cultural authenticity, a reclamation of indigenous identity, and a demonstration of linguistic pride in contemporary India, reflecting a broader national trend of post-colonial self-assertion.

Conclusion: Awaiting the Final Chapter in Kerala's Renaming Journey

The Union Cabinet's approval of the proposed name change of Kerala to Keralam signifies a monumental step towards realizing a long-cherished aspiration for the people of this vibrant state. Rooted in linguistic pride, historical accuracy, and a desire to shed colonial vestiges, the move to adopt 'Keralam' reflects a deeper cultural reclamation. While the process has moved through unanimous state legislative resolutions and central government endorsement, the final chapter awaits parliamentary approval and the President's assent.

This ongoing journey underscores the dynamic nature of India's federal structure, where cultural identity and historical continuity often drive significant administrative and constitutional changes.
